## POST 1

**Title:** Counting Carbs in a Thali: A Plate-by-Plate Guide

**Category:** Recipes

**Cover image URL:**
`https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1742281257707-0c7f7e5ca9c6?auto=format&fit=crop&q=70&w=1200`

**Excerpt:**
A full Indian thali can look impossible to count — rice, dal, sabzi, roti, a sweet.
Here's how to break it down into real, usable carb numbers.

**Content:**

A thali is one of the most balanced meals in the world — and one of the most
intimidating to carb-count, because there's so much on the plate at once. The trick
is to stop looking at it as one big meal and start seeing it as its parts.

## Start with the "big three" carbs

On most thalis, the bulk of your carbohydrate comes from just three things:

- **Rice** — about 45g of carbs per cooked cup
- **Roti / chapati** — about 15g each
- **The sweet** (gulab jamun, kheer, etc.) — often 30g or more per small serving

Add those up first and you've usually captured 70–80% of the meal's carbs in under
a minute.

## Then the "hidden middle"

The dals and sabzis feel like the main event, but they're often lighter on carbs
than the rice beside them:

- **Dal** — around 20g per cup
- **Rajma or chana** — higher, around 40–45g per cup (they're legumes)
- **Most vegetable sabzis** — 10–25g per cup, depending on the vegetable and gravy

## A worked example

A typical plate — one cup of rice, two rotis, a cup of dal, a cup of aloo sabzi,
and one gulab jamun — lands around **145g of carbs**. Knowing that number is the
difference between guessing and managing.

## The shortcut

You don't need to weigh anything forever. Once you've counted your five or six
regular thali combinations a couple of times, you'll recognize them by sight. That's
exactly what the app's food database is for — search the dish, get the estimate, and
move on with your day.

*None of this is a substitute for advice from your doctor or dietitian, especially
if you count carbs for insulin dosing.*

## POST 2

**Title:** "Sugar-Free" Doesn't Mean "Carb-Free": 5 Label Myths

**Category:** Myth-busting

**Cover image URL:**
`https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1512621776951-a57141f2eefd?auto=format&fit=crop&q=70&w=1200`

**Excerpt:**
The front of the package says one thing; the nutrition panel says another. Five
label claims that fool people managing diabetes — and what to check instead.

**Content:**

Food labels are marketing first and information second. For anyone watching their
blood sugar, a few common claims cause real confusion. Here are five worth knowing.

## Myth 1: "Sugar-free" means it won't raise your glucose

Sugar-free only means no *added* sugars — the product can still be full of
carbohydrate from flour, starch, or sugar alcohols, all of which affect glucose.
**Check the total carbohydrate line, not the sugar line.**

## Myth 2: "No added sugar" means low carb

A carton of fruit juice can proudly say "no added sugar" and still deliver 26g of
carbs a cup from the fruit itself. Natural sugar is still sugar to your body.

## Myth 3: "Whole grain" means you can eat freely

Whole grains are a better choice for fiber and steadier digestion — but a whole-grain
bread slice still carries roughly the same carbs as a white one. Better quality,
similar quantity.

## Myth 4: "Diabetic-friendly" is a regulated term

In most places it isn't. It's a marketing phrase, not a standard. Ignore the front
of the box and read the actual numbers on the back.

## Myth 5: Sugar alcohols are "free"

Sweeteners like maltitol and sorbitol have less impact than sugar, but they're not
zero — and in large amounts they can still nudge glucose (and upset your stomach).

## The one habit that beats all five

Flip the package over and read the **total carbohydrate per serving**, then check the
**serving size** — the number that's quietly "per ¼ of the package." That single
habit cuts through nearly every label trick.

*This is general education, not personalized dietary advice — your care team can help
you set targets that fit you.*
